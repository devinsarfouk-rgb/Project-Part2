What Is Phishing?

Phishing is a confidence trick that targets people, not systems. It uses trust, urgency and familiarity to make you act before you stop and check. Research shows that users learn safer habits faster when warnings explain exactly why a message is suspicious (Evaluating User Awareness of Phishing Threats, 2024).

Impersonation

Attackers pretend to be banks, online services or colleagues by copying logos, email headers, and website design. If it looks familiar, many people stop looking for the real warning signs.

Urgency & Fear

Messages pressure you with deadlines and threats: "Verify now or lose access." That rush is exactly what makes the scam work, because it shuts down careful decision-making.

Deceptive Links

Link text can look real while the actual address goes somewhere dangerous. Checking the real URL is one of the best ways to spot a fake page.

How a Phishing Attack Works
1
Attacker selects a target

Could be massdistributed or carefully targeted for example by using spear phishing, and using information gathered from social media or data leaks.

2
Crafts a convincing message

Copies brand logos, uses correct terminology, references real transactions or events, and exploits psychological triggers.

3
Victim clicks a link or attachment

Directed to a fake login webpage or downloads malware. The webpage looks identical to the real site.

4
Credentials harvested or malware installed

Attacker gains access to accounts, financial data, or deploys ransomware across the network.

5
Damage & escalation

Account takeover, financial theft, data breach, ransomware deployment, or lateral movement through an organisation's network.

Types of Phishing Attacks

Phishing can arrive as broad spam or a highly focused scam. The delivery changes, but the goal is always the same: steal access, money, or sensitive data.

Email Phishing

Mass emails impersonate familiar brands and hope enough people take the bait. Even a tiny click rate can lead to thousands of compromised accounts.

Spear Phishing

This attack is personalised for a specific person or organisation. It uses real names, roles and details found online to make the message feel genuine.

Whaling

Targeting senior leaders means the payoff is high. An email that looks like it comes from a CEO or CFO can authorize large fraud payments.

Smishing (SMS)

Texts appear urgent and personal. A fake delivery notice or bank alert can feel normal, so people often trust it without checking the sender.

Vishing (Voice)

Attackers call pretending to be your bank or IT support. Spoofed caller IDs and friendly scripts make the call seem legitimate.

Clone Phishing

Here the attacker reuses a real email you already received, but swaps the safe links for dangerous ones. Familiarity makes it easier to trust.

Search Engine Phishing

Fake pages rank in search results for common services. People assume the site is genuine because it appeared through a search engine.

Business Email Compromise

A hacked or spoofed executive email instructs finance or HR to transfer money. It often looks like routine internal communication.

AI-Generated Phishing

AI tools now write phishing emails that sound natural, polite and very specific. They remove the obvious spelling and tone mistakes attackers used to make.

📈 How Phishing Has Evolved

Phishing started as obvious spam, but it has become much more sophisticated. Today’s attacks use familiar brands, personal details, and AI-generated language to trick even cautious users.

1990s — Early Phishing
AOL Account Theft
The term "phishing" originates from hackers using fake AOL instant messages to steal account credentials. Early attacks were unsophisticated and easily spotted by careful users.
2000s — Banking & Mass Email
Financial Institution Targeting
Attackers shifted to targeting banks. Mass email campaigns impersonating Barclays, PayPal, and eBay began. Fake websites copied real designs but lacked HTTPS.
2010s — Spear Phishing & APTs
Targeted Corporate Attacks
Nation-state actors began using spear phishing for espionage (e.g., the 2016 DNC hack). Attacks became personalised using LinkedIn and social media data. HTTPS adoption made detection harder.
2018–2022 — Deepfakes & COVID
Crisis-Exploiting Attacks
Phishing attacks surged 220% during COVID-19, exploiting fear and uncertainty. Voice cloning ("vishing") and deepfake video calls began impersonating executives to authorise transfers.
2023–Present — AI-Powered
The LLM Phishing Revolution
ChatGPT and similar LLMs allow attackers to generate perfect, personalised phishing at industrial scale. QR code phishing ("quishing") bypasses email filters. MFA bypass toolkits commoditise advanced attacks.
Why AI Makes Phishing Harder to Detect

Today’s phishing is not just obvious spelling mistakes and poor design. AI tools can craft personalised, believable messages that match your name, company and recent activity, making scams much harder to spot.

That means looking only at how an email or webpage appears is no longer enough. Instead, users need clear guidance on why a message is suspicious and what specific cues to check.

Research shows explainable alerts that highlight dangerous features help people make better decisions, especially when the scam looks legitimate (Roy, Torres & Nilizadeh, 2025).

📊 Research Key Finding

Recent research (Roy, Torres & Nilizadeh, 2025) shows that generic security warnings are not enough. Users are far more likely to act safely when the alert explains exactly which features made the page or email suspicious.

Another study on phishing awareness found many people still trust a message because it looks familiar, instead of checking the URL, sender and context first (Evaluating User Awareness of Phishing Threats, 2024). That means good security design must teach, not just warn.

How to Protect Yourself

A combination of technical measures and behavioural habits provides the strongest defence against phishing.

✓ What You Should Do
  • Always check the full URL before entering credentials — hover over links to preview the destination
  • Verify requests by contacting the organisation directly through their official website, not via email links
  • Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) on all accounts, especially email and banking
  • Use a password manager — it won't autofill credentials on fake sites
  • Keep software, browsers, and antivirus up to date
  • Report suspicious emails to your IT department and to the NCSC (report@phishing.gov.uk)
  • Check that websites use HTTPS and that the padlock appears before any domain you trust
  • Use this toolkit or similar tools to scan suspicious URLs or emails before interacting with them
✗ What to Avoid
  • Never click links in unexpected emails — go directly to the website by typing the URL
  • Never provide your password, PIN, or full card number via email, phone, or text — no legitimate organisation asks for these
  • Do not trust an email just because it looks professional or has a familiar logo
  • Never open unexpected attachments, especially .html, .exe, .zip, or .js files
  • Don't assume HTTPS means safe — phishing sites can and do obtain SSL certificates
  • Avoid using public Wi-Fi for banking or sensitive logins without a VPN
  • Don't reuse passwords — a single breach can compromise multiple accounts

⚖ Legal & Regulatory Landscape

Despite efforts, phishing remains difficult to prosecute due to the anonymous, borderless nature of cyberspace.

🇬🇧

Computer Misuse Act 1990

UK legislation criminalising unauthorised computer access. Phishing falls under Section 2 (unauthorised access with intent to commit further offences). Carries up to 5 years imprisonment.

GDPR & Data Protection

Organisations that suffer phishing-enabled data breaches face significant GDPR fines of up to 4% of global annual turnover. This has driven investment in security training.

The Enforcement Gap

Most phishers operate across international boundaries. Attribution is difficult, and many jurisdictions have weak cybercrime enforcement — meaning most attackers are never prosecuted.

NCSC & DMARC

The UK's NCSC has championed DMARC email authentication — reducing UK government domain spoofing by 99%. Organisations implementing DMARC, DKIM, and SPF significantly reduce phishing risk.

Takedown Challenges

Phishing sites are typically active for only hours before being reported and taken down. Attackers register dozens of backup domains in advance, maintaining availability through rapid rotation.

Industry Response

Google Safe Browsing, Microsoft Defender, and browser-based phishing detection now block millions of malicious URLs daily — but sophisticated, newly-registered phishing sites evade these systems initially.
